This class forces you to take learning into your own hands. A very independent class were you actively have to seek help if you need it but offers many great recourses if you do. Everyone is very down to earth and willing to help if needed.
This course is manageable and enjoyable if you make sure to stay on top of your work. There are 3 problem sets a week and class 4 days a week – which is a big commitment! But if you can create a schedule for yourself, you will learn the content and succeed. It is a good idea to do the textbook readings before class because class is mostly focused on completing a worksheet and not on instruction. I also highly recommend completing practice exams –– they are generally harder than the real exams, but are great practice. Start preparing for the exams well in advance because each covers a good bit of content. Also, go to office hours! Especially with Janet Chen, who is a fantastic instructor and really patient when explaining the concepts. Make sure to work with other students too (and credit them when you do)–– Math21a is a huge class so it is a great chance to collaborate and learn from your peers.

Do not feel intimidated by this class if you don't have the strongest grasp of BC Calculus. As long as you have a relatively solid grasp of integrals and are willing to review some as you go, you should be fine. Personally, I did not fully learn parametric equations, polar coordinates, and infinite sequences and series in BC Calculus due to COVID. I was worried this would lead me to fall behind in Math21a, but it was not an issue.

Finally, if you don't have to take this course for a concentration, make sure you are actually enthusiastic about the topics because this class will take up a good bit of time!
If you want to learn multi, this is the course. You really do learn so so much because it moves very quickly. Yes, 3 psets is a lot but starting early (literally right after class) was the move. Section is also really good because you get to practice with CAs answering any questions you have. If you do take it, actually try to understand the hws and handouts because they are literally the kinds of problems you see on the test. And practice is so key! A lot of the concepts are hard to visualize/comprehend but sometimes just repetition and getting familiar with using them are how you learn them. USE OFFICE HOURS IF YOU CAN INSTEAD OF MQC!! While MQC is super helpful, your professors give you more individualized attention during office hours and it's way less intense than MQC.
