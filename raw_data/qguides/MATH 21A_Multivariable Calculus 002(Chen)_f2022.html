What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
If you are willing to spend a moderate amount of pretty much every single day on math, this course is for you – it's not too vigorous in its course material but rather gets you familiar with the concepts by hammering you with 3 problem sets per week. Some of the first friends I made at Harvard were from study groups in Math 21a; I think it's a good continuation off of IB / AP programs and it's efficient if you're planning on concentrating in Chemistry / Economics.
PSET with other students if you decide to take the course. Go to the Math Question Center for help if you need it, they're a good resource. There is no lecture, just section, and the class is very computationally/visually intensive (lots of churning numbers and visualizing 3d shapes). Content is manageable but the structure of the course can detract from retaining information.
For exams, do all of the practice problems.
Specifically for physics concentrators: I would suggest taking this course before physics 15b rather than concurrently if you can, as I struggled with early components in physics that I only fully understood 2/3 through math 21a.
Take this course only if you have done multivariable calculus in high school or are a genius who can figure out math concepts without them being taught. This course essentially expects you to teach yourself the material. Also, make sure that you get a competent TF. I did not learn anything until I switched sections halfway through the year.

I’m not saying this is a bad course, but I would have liked to know this going in.
Do not take this as your 9 A.M.
Do not take this class unless you need multivariable calculus for your studies (ie physics or engineering). The work in this class is excessive and will take up a lot of your time. If your section sucks like mine did make sure to go to MQC and office hours.
This course was an excellent overview of multivariable calculus. Although it does not provide the most rigorous approach, it is highly digestible and helps build the ability to approach and solve problems. I would recommend this course for anyone who is interested in learning multivariable calculus and how it can be applied.
If you're an independent learner you should be fine. The content is not particularly difficult (not any harder than single variable calculus), but you do have 3 problem sets a week and class 4 times a week (section + workshop) so it will take up some of your time.
If you are planning/required to take a math series, I would recommend taking Math 21a. Pros: 1) lots of people take the class so you will have people to work with; 2) because lots of people take the class, there is lots of support infrastructure, such as the Math Question Center and accessible Section Leaders; Cons: 1) sections move quite fast and, depending on your leader, will not always teach (you will learn through problem sets), 2) with such a big course, the heads are not always helpful when you have extenuating circumstances.
