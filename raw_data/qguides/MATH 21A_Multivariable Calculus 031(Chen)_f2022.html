Please don't take this class unless you have to. It won't be a fun elective gem. I spent anywhere from 10–20 hours per week on this class and had to go out of my way to teach myself a lot of the material. There are 3 long psets a week and if you don't get a TF that's excellent at teaching you will probably struggle, unless you have a background in multivariable or just have no problem spending a lot of time outside of class learning it. The exams are also weighted extremely heavily in your final grade and there are only 3 of them, including the final, so be very careful and start studying for them early. Doing the practice exams helps a lot.
Super fun!
Do NOT take this course if you think it’s going to be easier than math 1b it’s a trick. The course is hard and the problem set grading is a gamble in the dark.
Very solid course. You will learn a lot and do well in the course, provided that you don't let yourself get behind. The way the course was run this year made it very fast–paced, so you have to be on top of it every day.
This class requires a few hours more than the other classes you are enrolled in, especially if you are a bit rusty in your calculus knowledge. Be prepared to attend MQC to get help on PSET questions, I recommend attending the day it is assigned as there are fewer people those days and you can receive more help one–on–one. Regardless, the class was very fun, especially being able to visualize a lot of things in 3d.
This course is difficult and a good amount of work, but still manageable. Office hours and MQC are extremely helpful resources to take advantage of. Don't be afraid to ask questions to your TF and CAs in section.
I would tell future students to attend MQC regularly to make sure they understand the foundational concept and not just memorize the problems.
This is an excellent course, and the course staff are super invested in the learning experience. The problem sets are three times a week and can be quite beefy sometimes, so this class will take up a significant amount of time—but nowhere near as much as 22/25/55. Go to MQC for help with problem sets—you'll never get a question wrong if you go consistently. Take this class over 18a/19a—from what I've heard, 21a is better structured compared to those two, and you'll have no problem applying 21a course concepts to the life or social sciences because a lot of the problem set/exam/class problems deal with economic or science–y scenarios.
I would strongly recommend Math 21a. There are a lot of psets (3 a week) but they are generally short and don't take too long (less than 2 hours each). The structure of the class helps you really understand the material. You need a good understanding of calculus to take the class.
Do as many practice tests as you can! They are an excellent resource that you can use to prepare for real exams! Also, go to MQC, even if you think you did everything on the PSET correctly because it's always best to check your understanding with the TAs and your peers. There's no reason you shouldn't be getting A's on the PSETS!
