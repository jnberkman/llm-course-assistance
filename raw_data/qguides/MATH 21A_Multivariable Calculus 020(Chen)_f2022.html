Review the worksheets. They are the key to mastery of whatever concept you might struggle with throughout this course.
Math 21a is so incredibly well–taught. It's difficult content, but class time is always used very effectively and the problem sets are manageable.
Take this class! It is the best math class that I have taken at Harvard. They focus on conceptual understanding and provide through explanations. Moreover, they provide many practice problems and exams for you to tackle in preparation for problem sets or exams. Staff is also always willing to help.
This course has a great teaching style. Smaller sections allowed you to work through problems, try to figure things out, and then the TF explains everything and tells you the right way to do things. I felt like I learned things really well because of this. The grading policy of this course also felt like they really wanted us to succeed because of the ways things were weighted differently depending on how well you did on them (between the final and midterm the higher score was worth 35% of your grade and the lower score 25%). I also liked that they motivated you to start studying early for every exam by offering late days for completing practice tests, so you're more prepared going into them. The 3 psets a week they did this semester seemed like a lot at first, but if you get a good pset group/buddy, you can easily just get sit down and get each one done in like 90 minutes tops. Also if you have taken calc before, don't stress if you struggled with series because you really only need a good understanding of derivatives and integrals because this class is mainly about those things, but in 3d.
Brace yourselves!! This is no easy class, even if you have a strong mathematical background. Good luck on the exams.
21a used to be a pretty easy class but this semester, there are 3 psets/week and 4 meetings/week with attendance being a part of your grade. It was difficult to keep up with, the classes themselves weren’t particularly inspiring, and as a math major taking 3 pset classes, I dreaded 21a every day
If you put in the work doing the psets and worksheets in class, this class is definitely manageable. Unlike some students in the class, I had never taken multivariable calculus before, but I still felt comfortable. Would definitely recommend!
Be prepared for the workload
Class is well–structured but the work is tedious. If you want to learn multivariable calculus, you'll enjoy the class.
This class is very thorough and connects nicely. If you don't have prior multi experience, going to section and paying attention the whole time is very important. Then, just doing the psets and understanding them will reinforce the concepts enough for you to be very proud of the multivariable calculus you've learned.
I am not a huge math person so I wouldn't exactly call this class fun, but I honestly enjoyed this more than any other class I've taken. Math 21a did a great job of teaching more than the bare minimum of multivariable calculus without going overboard. I really liked the structure overall, and Janet Chen is an amazing course head. She is kind, understanding, and of course, teaches math well.

Advice for students: make sure you budget time to do the psets, it took between 2.5–4 hours usually to finish one and there are 3 per week. Go to section– it's much easier to learn in class than on your own (and they take attendance). Also, go to office hours and/or MQC if you have a question or just to do the pset (you'll finish the psets faster!)
This course is a great course to take after Single–Variable calculus and teaches very foundational concepts.
The course is run effectively and is very well–organized. Review and learn the material after every section and I promise you will do well. 3 psets a week can be tough though.
The TFs for this course are amazing! The 3 class, 3 pset, 1 workshop a week structure can be taxing at times, but you definitely get good at what you learn/practice and the length of the psets are not too bad. Math 21A does a great job of being flexible with students (lots of support, many office hours, many practice materials, shorter psets before exams, etc). I loved the community and people I met in section or checking pset answers with. Whether you have taken multi before or not, most people come out with really well developed multivariable calculus skills. The exams are always close to what you expect (they want you to succeed) and the course is organized. If you want to get a good foundation in multi AND understand it well, this is a fun course to take. Just make sure you're mentally ready for the workload and commitment.
I truly enjoyed this class, but it was not easy. I would recommend finding a comfortable pset group early in the semester, as you will be working with them 30+ throughout the term. I appreciated the smaller scale of lectures and the various support systems. Overall, I had a good time.
I would tell future students to, if they choose to take this course, make sure that this is your most difficult course. This course requires time. The material is not difficult necessarily, there are just so many ways to approach each problem that practice and solid foundations are necessary. Do not take this course at the same time as Organic Chemistry. Do not do it. I repeat. Do not do it. You do not want to be struggling to figure out which class to pour your time into. Going to MQC and working on problem sets 3 times a week will take away from time that you may need to put into other classes. Make sure that you have the time for this class and that you can dedicate yourself to this class.
